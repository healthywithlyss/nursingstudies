Upper vs. Lower Airway

Upper airway (above larynx): nasal passages, mouth, pharynx — NOT sterile

Lower airway (below larynx): trachea, bronchi, bronchioles — STERILE (critical for suctioning)

Alveoli = site of gas exchange

Ventilation / Respiration / Oxygenation

Ventilation = movement of air in/out of lungs (breathing)

Respiration = exchange of O2/CO2

  • External: at lungs/alveoli — O2 enters blood, CO2 exits

  • Internal: at body organs & tissues — O2 leaves blood into cells, CO2 returns

Oxygenation = how well O2 reaches cells, tissues, organs

You cannot measure gas exchange by counting breaths per minute.

Hypoxemia vs. Hypoxia

Hypoxemia = low O2 in BLOOD (PaO2 <80 mmHg) — poor alveolar diffusion

Hypoxia = low O2 in TISSUES (SpO2 <90%) — hypoxemia or circulation issue

Hypoxemia → hypoxia if untreated

Hypercarbia vs. Hypocarbia

Hypercarbia PaCO2 >45 = excess CO2 — hypoventilation — anesthetic effect → drowsiness → coma → death

Hypocarbia PaCO2 <35 = low CO2 — hyperventilation — stimulating effect → tingling, cramps, dizziness

⚠ COPD Hypoxic Drive: Normal: HIGH CO2 = primary stimulus to breathe. COPD: Chronically ↑ CO2 → body desensitized → LOW O2 = only stimulus. Too much O2 → removes drive → patient STOPS BREATHING → DEATH. Use Venturi mask. Target SpO2 88–92%.
Respiratory Assessment — Inspection: 6 Characteristics

1. Skin & Mucous Membrane Color

Pale, cyanotic, or normal; inspect throat, palate, tonsils, pharynx, nose

2. Cough & Sputum — COCAF

  • Color: white/clear=viral · yellow/green=bacterial · pink frothy=pulm. edema · rust=TB · black=smoke
  • Odor: foul = bacterial (pneumonia, lung abscess)
  • Consistency: thick/tenacious vs. watery
  • Amount  ·  Frequency

3. Respiratory Rate, Rhythm, Depth, Pattern, Effort

Note all 5 together

4. Symmetry of Chest Movements

Asymmetry = problem: unilateral obstruction, pleural fibrosis, effusion, pneumothorax

5. Shape of Chest — AP Diameter

Normal AP:Lateral = 1:2

Barrel chest 1:1 = COPD/emphysema

6. Spinal Deformities & Edema

Kyphosis = posterior curve (common elderly) · Scoliosis = lateral S-curve · note presence/absence of edema

Palpation & Percussion

Palpation (FEEL)

Assess: pain/tenderness · masses · respiratory excursion · skin temperature

Respiratory Excursion: Thumbs at T10 — both sides should expand equally; asymmetry = pleurisy, fractured ribs, trauma

Percussion

Resonance = normal lung  ·  Dullness = fluid/pneumonia/solid organs

Hyperresonance = emphysema/pneumothorax  ·  Flatness = bone/solid tissue

8 Signs of Respiratory Distress & Signs of Hypoxia

8 Signs of Respiratory Distress/Effort

  1. Nasal flaring — nostrils enlarge to ↓ airway resistance
  2. Retractions — skin pulls in between/below ribs
  3. Accessory muscle use — neck, shoulder, abdomen
  4. Grunting — forced expiration to keep alveoli open
  5. Orthopnea — SOB lying flat; must sit upright
  6. Conversational dyspnea — can't complete sentences
  7. Wheezing — narrowed small airways — EXPIRATORY
  8. Stridor ⚠ — crowing — INSPIRATORY — EMERGENCY

Early Signs of Hypoxia — ACT NOW

  • Restlessness (FIRST SIGN) · Anxiety · Confusion
  • Tachycardia · Tachypnea · Hypertension
  • Nasal flaring · Accessory muscle use · Pale skin
  • Adventitious lung sounds

Late Signs of Hypoxia — LIFE-THREATENING

  • Bradycardia · Bradypnea · Extreme restlessness
  • Severe dyspnea · Decreased LOC/Coma · Hypotension
  • Cyanosis of tongue & oral mucosa = best indicator
  • Chronic hypoxia → clubbing of fingers/toes

COPD — Chronic Bronchitis vs. Emphysema
"Blue Bloater" — Chronic Bronchitis

Problem: Inflammation + mucus hypersecretion in bronchi → O2 can't get IN

Etiology: Smoking (90%), occupational exposures, air pollution, asthma

S&S: Chronic productive cough · thick tenacious sputum · rhonchi · wheezing · hypoxemia/cyanosis · tachycardia · tachypnea · dyspnea · peripheral edema · orthopnea

Dx: PFTs · CXR · ABGs

TX: Bronchodilators · corticosteroids · expectorants · controlled O2/BiPAP · stop smoking · pulm. rehab · vaccinations

"Pink Puffer" — Emphysema

Problem: Destruction of alveoli + air trapping → CO2 can't get OUT

Etiology: Smoking (90%), occupational exposures, air pollution

S&S: Barrel chest · pursed-lip breathing · difficulty exhaling · grunting · weight loss · tripod positioning · clubbing · accessory muscle use · infrequent cough · orthopnea · dyspnea

Dx: PFTs · CXR · ABGs

TX: Same as bronchitis + pursed-lip breathing is critical

Blue Bloater: cyanotic, overweight, mucus IN airways, O2 can't get IN, rhonchi  |  Pink Puffer: pink, thin, air TRAPPED, CO2 can't get OUT, pursed-lip breathing to release CO2
Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

Signs & Symptoms

  • Snoring/gasping/restlessness during sleep
  • Apnea lasting 10–120 seconds
  • Unrefreshing sleep · daytime sleepiness
  • Morning headache (CO2 retained)
  • Dry mouth in AM

Risk Factors

  • Age >40 · male · overweight
  • Neck >17" (M) / >16" (F)
  • Alcohol before sleep · smoking
  • Sleep-inducing medications
  • Large tonsils/tongue, small mandible

Treatment

  • CPAP (first line) · BiPAP
  • Side-lying positioning
  • Weight loss of at least 10%
  • No alcohol · no smoking
  • Dental appliances
⚠ Sedatives, opioids, benzos, anesthesia = DANGEROUS in OSA

Suctioning — Yankauer vs. Suction Catheter

Yankauer (Upper Airway)

  • Suctions mouth/throat — NOT sterile
  • Used for gurgling or moist sounds in upper airway
  • PRN only — not a routine procedure

Suction Catheter (Lower Airway — STERILE)

  • Pre-oxygenate with 100% O2 before suctioning
  • Max 10 seconds per pass — suction on withdrawal ONLY
  • Max 3 passes per session · allow recovery time between passes
  • Complications: mucosal trauma · hypoxia · bronchospasm · atelectasis · dysrhythmias
Nursing Interventions for Gaseous Transfer

Positioning

High Fowler's or orthopneic/tripod — gravity pulls abdominal organs down, maximizes diaphragmatic excursion

Incentive Spirometer (INHALE)

Prevents atelectasis · slow deep inhale, hold 3–5 sec · 10–20x/hr · used for surgical/immobilized patients

Pursed-Lip Breathing (EXHALE)

Slows breathing down · keeps airways open longer to release trapped CO2 · improves O2/CO2 exchange · key for emphysema

Mobilize Secretions

Coughing, deep breathing, chest PT · maintain hydration to thin secretions · postural drainage · percussion · vibration

Peak Flow Meter (EXHALE — Asthma)

Green 80–100% = all clear
Yellow 50–80% = take bronchodilator
Red <50% = go to ED

Health Promotion & Teaching

Smoking cessation · diet & exercise · vaccines (flu, pneumonia) · infection control · occupational safety

Medications: Codeine & Guaifenesin

Codeine

Classification: Antitussive (opioid) — controlled substance

Action: Suppresses cough reflex in medulla

Key Nursing: Monitor RR & sedation level · SE: sedation, constipation, hypotension · do NOT give with CNS depressants · caution in COPD/OSA

Guaifenesin (Mucinex)

Classification: Expectorant

Action: Thins/loosens mucus → more productive cough — does NOT suppress cough — promotes it

Key Nursing: Encourage ↑ fluid intake (essential for drug to work) · assess sputum · give with full glass of water

Codeine = SUPPRESS cough (antitussive)  ·  Guaifenesin = PROMOTE cough (expectorant)

Key Definitions

Circulation = blood flowing through heart and vessels

Perfusion = O2 reaching capillary beds and tissues

Ischemia = tissue alive, O2-deprived — reversible if treated

Infarction (MI) = tissue DEAD — irreversible

Conduction System

SA Node — 60–100 bpm · primary pacemaker · both atria

AV Node — slight delay → if SA fails, AV takes over

Bundle of His → Bundles → Purkinje → ventricles

Both fail → ventricular conduction <40 bpm

Blood Flow

Body → Vena Cava → R. Atrium → Tricuspid → R. Ventricle → Pulmonic → Lungs → Pulm. Vein → L. Atrium → Mitral → L. Ventricle → Aortic → Body

Atria RECEIVE · Ventricles PUMP

EKG Waves

P wave = atrial depolarization → contract

QRS = ventricular depolarization → contract

T wave = ventricular repolarization → relax

Flat T = hypokalemia · Tall T = hyperkalemia · Absent P = A-fib

ANS — Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic

Sympathetic ("Fight or Flight")

  • ↑ HR + ↑ contractility
  • Neurotransmitters: epinephrine & norepinephrine
  • Beta receptors (atenolol is a beta blocker → blocks this effect)
  • Controls ALL blood vessels — vasoconstriction at rest = vascular tone

Parasympathetic ("Rest & Digest")

  • ↓ HR ONLY via vagus nerve
  • NO effect on contractility
  • NO significant vessel control
  • Neurotransmitter: acetylcholine

Heart Failure — Left vs. Right
Left HF — "LEFT = LUNGS" (Most Common)

Etiology: LV can't pump → blood backs up into LUNGS → pulmonary congestion

S&S: Cough · SOB/dyspnea · orthopnea · crackles · wheezing · pink frothy sputum · tachypnea · tachycardia · confusion · weakness · fatigue · exercise intolerance · lack of appetite

Key: BNP ↑ = LV stretched = HF severity · EF normal 50–75%

Right HF — "RIGHT = REST OF BODY"

Etiology: RV can't pump → blood backs up into PERIPHERAL VEINS → systemic congestion (often caused by left HF)

S&S: Peripheral edema · JVD · ascites · weight gain · enlarged liver/spleen · fatigue · weakness · exercise intolerance · lack of appetite

Right = Rings (edema so rings don't fit)

PAD — Peripheral Arterial Disease

Problem: Plaque narrows arteries → not enough blood reaching legs (arterial insufficiency)

S&S: Pale/cyanotic · cool · weak/absent pulses · no leg hair · thick toenails · ischemic wounds on toes · intermittent claudication (leg pain with walking, relieved by rest)

Nursing: Keep legs DOWN — gravity assists arterial flow · daily foot care · smoking cessation · no restrictive clothing

PVD — Peripheral Venous Disease

Problem: Damaged/incompetent veins → blood POOLS in legs (venous insufficiency)

S&S: Brownish-red discoloration · varicose veins · pitting edema · stasis ulcers on lower leg

Nursing: Keep legs UP — gravity drains blood to heart · ambulation · compression stockings · no crossing legs · ankle circles/calf pumps

PAD legs DOWN  ·  PVD legs UP

ABG & Acid-Base Balance Lecture 11

☑ Objectives

3 ways body maintains acid-base balance · Normal values: pH, PaCO2, HCO3− · Analyze ABGs for all 4 disorders (resp. acidosis/alkalosis, met. acidosis/alkalosis) · Compensation NOT required for exam

3 Ways the Body Maintains Acid-Base Balance
1. Buffers — 1st & Fastest

Act instantly — no organ required. Neutralize acids/bases in blood and tissue.

Carbonic acid-bicarbonate (90% ECF), phosphate (ICF), protein systems

2. Lungs — Control CO2 (Minutes)

Too acidic → breathe fast, blow off CO2  |  Too alkaline → breathe slow, retain CO2

When metabolic is the cause → lungs compensate

3. Kidneys — Control HCO3− (Days)

Too acidic → retain HCO3−, excrete H+ into urine

Too alkaline → excrete HCO3−  |  When respiratory is the cause → kidneys compensate

Normal ABG Values  |  pH Scale

pH 7.35–7.45 PaCO2 35–45 mmHg HCO3− 22–26 mEq/L PaO2 80–100 mmHg

<7.35 = ACIDOSIS  ·  7.35–7.45 = NORMAL  ·  >7.45 = ALKALOSIS

CO2 is an ACID — moves OPPOSITE to pH  |  HCO3− is a BASE — moves SAME as pH

ROME: Respiratory = Opposite  ·  Metabolic = Equal

Tic-Tac-Toe Method — How to Analyze an ABG

The 3 Steps

Step 1: Is pH acid (<7.35), normal, or base (>7.45)?

Step 2: Is PaCO2 acid (>45), normal, or base (<35)?

Step 3: Is HCO3− acid (<22), normal, or base (>26)?

Whichever value matches the pH direction = the CAUSE

CO2 matches → Respiratory  |  HCO3− matches → Metabolic

Clinical Clues

  • COPD + altered LOC → respiratory acidosis
  • CHF/pneumonia, hypoventilating → respiratory acidosis
  • Anxious patient hyperventilating → respiratory alkalosis → tingling
  • Sepsis/fever → respiratory alkalosis
  • DKA patient breathing rapidly and deeply → Kussmaul's → metabolic acidosis
  • Severe diarrhea → metabolic acidosis (losing HCO3−)
  • Post-op on opioids, hypoventilating → respiratory acidosis
  • Vomiting/NG suction → losing stomach acid → metabolic alkalosis
  • Furosemide without K+ replacement → hypokalemia → metabolic alkalosis
  • ASA overdose → metabolic acidosis
Fluids & Electrolytes Lecture 11

☑ Objectives

Fluid compartments (ICF, ECF: interstitial, intravascular, transcellular) · Osmosis & diffusion · 10 S&S of need for fluids · ADH role in fluid balance · Hypovolemia vs. hypervolemia (S&S & NI) · Fluid restriction & increasing interventions

Fluid Compartments

Intracellular Fluid (ICF) — Largest compartment

Inside all body cells

Major ions: K+, Mg2+, phosphate−

Third Spacing ⚠ — fluid trapped where body cannot use it: ascites, pericardial effusion, burn blisters, pleural effusion. Circulating volume ↓ even though fluid hasn't left the body.

Extracellular Fluid (ECF) — 3 Subtypes

Interstitial — between cells & blood vessels → where edema forms

Intravascular — plasma inside blood vessels · main ions: Na+, Cl−, HCO3−, albumin

Transcellular — CSF, pleural fluid, peritoneal, synovial, digestive juices

Movement Mechanisms

Osmosis

Water moves from LOW → HIGH concentration (passive — no ATP)

Goal: dilute higher solute concentration

Diffusion

Solutes move from HIGH → LOW concentration (passive)

Example: O2/CO2 exchange at alveoli

Filtration

Water + particles move from HIGH → LOW pressure (passive)

Example: arteries → capillaries

Active Transport

Electrolytes move LOW → HIGH concentration

Requires ATP — ONLY energy-requiring mechanism

IV Fluid Tonicity
ISOTONIC — No Fluid Shift

Same osmolality as blood — stays in ECF

Examples: 0.9% NS, Lactated Ringers (LR)

LR has electrolytes — given for whole blood loss from trauma, surgery, GI bleed

0.9% NS — used for fluid loss, dehydration, hypovolemia

⚠ Risk of fluid overload — monitor especially in CHF
HYPOTONIC — "Hippo" — Cells Swell

Lower osmolality — water moves INTO cells → cells swell

Examples: 0.45% NS, 0.33% NS, D5W

Used for: intracellular dehydration, DKA

⚠ NEVER with ↑ICP, stroke, or head injury — causes cerebral edema / brain swelling
HYPERTONIC — Cells Shrink

Higher osmolality — water OUT of cells → cells shrink

Examples: D5NS, D5 1/2NS, D5LR, D10W, 3% NS

Used for: cerebral edema, severe hyponatremia, volume expansion

⚠ Risk hypervolemia · must run SLOW · monitor blood sugar (glucose solutions)
Role of ADH in Fluid Balance

How ADH Works

Low fluid volume or ↓ BP → osmoreceptors in hypothalamus signal → posterior pituitary releases ADH → kidneys RETAIN water → ↑ blood volume → raises BP

High volume → ADH inhibited → kidneys release water

Also: Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS): Low fluid → kidneys release renin → angiotensin II → kidneys hold Na+ and water → ↑ BP · ACE inhibitors block this → ↓ BP

Clinical Significance

ADH released: Urine concentrated (dark, small), specific gravity ↑ >1.030

ADH suppressed: Urine dilute (pale, large), specific gravity ↓ <1.002

SIADH: Too much ADH → too much water retained → dilutional hyponatremia → confusion, seizures

Diabetes Insipidus: Too little ADH → massive water loss → dehydration → hypernatremia

Hypovolemia vs. Hypervolemia

Hypovolemia — 10 Signs of Need for Fluids

  1. Thirst (first sign!)
  2. Headache
  3. Fatigue
  4. Concentrated/decreased urine
  5. ↑ heart rate & low BP (thready pulse, tachycardia, hypotension)
  6. Dry mouth and eyes
  7. Lack of coordination
  8. Muscle cramps
  9. Constipation
  10. Extreme cases: weakness, trembling, lack of mental clarity

Also: Elevated temp · dry mucous membranes · poor skin turgor · capillary refill >3 sec · AMS · elevated BUN & Na+ · elevated Hct · elevated urine specific gravity

Hypervolemia — Signs & Symptoms

Causes: Excess fluid/Na+ intake · CHF · renal failure · liver failure · low albumin

  • Weight gain (rapid)
  • JVD · pitting/dependent edema
  • Crackles · dyspnea · ascites
  • Bounding pulse · hypertension · ↑ RR
  • Mental status changes (low Na+ or hypoxia)
  • Skin pale & cool

Labs: ↓ BUN, ↓ Hct, ↓ urine specific gravity (<1.002), ↓ Na+ (hemodilution)

Nursing Interventions

To INCREASE Fluid Intake (Hypovolemia)

  • Assess and offer fluid preferences
  • Encourage oral fluids · offer choices throughout day
  • IV fluids as ordered (isotonic 0.9% NS)
  • Monitor I&O · daily weights · VS · mental status
  • Mouth care for dry mucous membranes
  • Always have fluids available

To RESTRICT Fluid Intake (Hypervolemia)

  • Fluid restriction as ordered — measure ALL intake
  • Reserve liquids for between meals — NOT with meals
  • Offer ice chips to manage thirst
  • Bring liquids for medications then take away
  • Do NOT leave liquids at the bedside
  • Diuretics as ordered (furosemide) · low-sodium diet
  • Monitor I&O · daily weights · lung sounds · HOB elevated

IV Access — Peripheral vs. Central
Peripheral IV

Catheter inserted into peripheral vein (arm/hand) for IV fluid/medication delivery

Gauge: Lower number = bigger lumen (18g > 22g)

Replace: Every 72 hours

✓ Easy · brief therapy · widely accessible

✗ Cannot use hyperosmolar or irritating solutions · fragile · dislodges easily

Central Venous Catheter (CVC) — Types

Non-tunneled CVC: Subclavian or jugular · MD at bedside · x-ray to verify placement BEFORE use

PICC Line: Antecubital fossa → superior vena cava · long-term · home use · RN assesses site AND catheter length every shift (length change = migration)

Implanted Port (Mediport): Under skin · long-term · accessed with Huber needle ONLY — sterile procedure

✓ Hyperosmolar solutions OK · TPN possible   ✗ MD insertion + consent + x-ray · ↑ infection risk

Blood Products — Indications for Transfusion

PRBCs

Raise Hgb/Hct · restore O2-carrying capacity · severe anemia · significant blood loss/trauma/hemorrhage

Platelets

Low platelet count · abnormal platelet function · clotting disorders

Plasma / Derivatives

Clotting factor deficits · immune deficiencies · albumin for low protein · WBCs for infections unresponsive to antibiotics

IV fluids restore fluid volume ONLY — cannot restore O2-carrying capacity or replace clotting factors. Only blood products can do this.
Transfusion — Time Limits & Setup

Pre-Transfusion Setup

  • Verify order
  • Consent signed by MD & patient — witnessed
  • Type & cross: valid 72 hrs — 4 blood types: A, B, AB, O — Rh factor: positive or negative
  • VS 5–15 min prior to administration
  • 18–20 gauge IV access · IV site must be patent & VS stable
  • ONLY 0.9% NS — dextrose causes RBC lysis
  • Y-tubing · if dextrose running → completely new IV line
  • Pre-meds if ordered: acetaminophen, diphenhydramine

Time Rules

Initiate within ½ hour of release from blood bank

Infuse in not less than 2 hours, not more than 4 hours

If not done in 4 hrs → stop & waste

2-nurse check required — ALWAYS

Stay with patient for first 15 minutes and retake vital signs

5 Types of Transfusion Reactions
Universal First Step — ALL reactions: STOP transfusion immediately and replace IV with 0.9% Normal Saline. No exceptions. Then identify type and notify MD.
ReactionCauseS/SNursing InterventionPreventable?
Febrile Sensitivity to WBCs, plasma proteins, platelets Fever · chills · warm flushed skin · aches STOP · NS · notify MD · acetaminophen · treat symptoms No
Allergic Allergy to blood transfused Flushing · itching · hives · wheezing · severe = anaphylaxis STOP · NS · antihistamine (mild) · epinephrine if anaphylaxis No
Bacterial ⚠ Contaminated blood product Fever · chills · vomiting · diarrhea · hypotension STOP · NS · notify MD · antibiotics as ordered No
Hemolytic ⚠⚠ Incompatible blood → RBC destruction (1 in 600,000) — WRONG blood to WRONG patient Fever · chills · dyspnea · chest pain · tachycardia · hypotension → FATAL STOP IMMEDIATELY · new NS via NEW tubing (not blood tubing) · notify MD STAT · hemolytic protocol · treat shock · send remaining blood + tubing + venous sample + first voided urine to lab YES — 2-nurse check
Circulatory Overload Too much volume or infused too fast Persistent cough · crackles · HTN · distended neck veins SLOW or STOP · sit patient upright · monitor VS · notify MD · anticipate Lasix (furosemide) YES — rate/volume control
Hemolytic = most serious AND most preventable. 2-nurse check confirms: right patient · right blood type · expiration date · bag ID. Never rush this check — 99% preventable.

Causes of Escalating Healthcare Costs

  • Aging population → more chronic conditions, LTC, specialty care
  • Higher survival rates → costly long-term/intensive care
  • Administrative waste from insurance system complexity
  • Lack of competition — hospital consolidation → unchecked price increases
  • Third-party payers insulate consumers from real cost (they don't see the bill)
  • Rising cost of medical technology and prescriptions
  • Overuse: unnecessary diagnostic tests · longer LOS
  • Cost of supplies, waste of supplies, complications patients experience
  • Cost of personnel

By 2028: $6.2 trillion projected (~$18,000/person). High cost ≠ high quality. US has worst maternal mortality in developed world; infant mortality rate 71% higher than comparable countries.

Nurses' Role in Cost Containment

Supplies:

  • Only bring needed supplies into room — unused room supplies must be discarded
  • Scan barcodes timely — capture all charges
  • Avoid unnecessary prepackaged kits

Practice:

  • Thorough assessments — catch reddened areas early → implement turn schedule → prevent pressure injuries
  • Infection control — CAUTI, VAP = preventable nosocomial infections that add to costs
  • Prioritize using Maslow → ABC → CURE
  • Delegate to UAPs appropriately
  • Cluster cares · document for continuity · update care plans
  • Individualize care based on current patient needs
ANA Resource Stewardship Standard: "The registered nurse utilizes appropriate resources to plan, provide, and sustain evidence-based nursing services that are safe, effective, financially responsible, and used judiciously."
