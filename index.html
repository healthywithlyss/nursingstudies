Upper vs. Lower Airway

Upper airway (above larynx): nasal passages, mouth, pharynx — NOT sterile

Lower airway (below larynx): trachea, bronchi, bronchioles — STERILE

Alveoli = site of gas exchange

Ventilation / Respiration / Oxygenation

Ventilation = movement of air in/out of lungs

External respiration: at lungs/alveoli — O2 enters blood, CO2 exits

Internal respiration: at tissues — O2 leaves blood into cells, CO2 returns

Oxygenation = how well O2 reaches cells, tissues, organs

Hypoxemia vs. Hypoxia

Hypoxemia = low O2 in BLOOD (PaO2 <80 mmHg)

Hypoxia = low O2 in TISSUES (SpO2 <90%) — hypoxemia or circulation issue

Hypercarbia vs. Hypocarbia

Hypercarbia PaCO2 >45 = excess CO2 — hypoventilation → drowsiness → coma

Hypocarbia PaCO2 <35 = low CO2 — hyperventilation → tingling, cramps, dizziness

⚠ COPD Hypoxic Drive: Normal: HIGH CO2 = primary stimulus to breathe. COPD: Chronically ↑ CO2 → body desensitized → LOW O2 = only stimulus. Too much O2 → removes drive → patient STOPS BREATHING. Use Venturi mask. Target SpO2 88–92%.

8 Signs of Respiratory Distress & Hypoxia

8 Signs of Respiratory Distress

  1. Nasal flaring
  2. Retractions — skin pulls in between/below ribs
  3. Accessory muscle use — neck, shoulder, abdomen
  4. Grunting — forced expiration to keep alveoli open
  5. Orthopnea — SOB lying flat
  6. Conversational dyspnea — can't complete sentences
  7. Wheezing — narrowed small airways — EXPIRATORY
  8. Stridor ⚠ — crowing — INSPIRATORY — EMERGENCY

Early Signs of Hypoxia

  • Restlessness (FIRST SIGN) · Anxiety · Confusion
  • Tachycardia · Tachypnea · Hypertension
  • Nasal flaring · Accessory muscle use · Pale skin
  • Adventitious lung sounds

Late Signs — LIFE-THREATENING

  • Bradycardia · Bradypnea · Extreme restlessness
  • Severe dyspnea · Decreased LOC/Coma · Hypotension
  • Cyanosis of tongue & oral mucosa = best indicator
  • Chronic hypoxia → clubbing of fingers/toes

COPD — Chronic Bronchitis vs. Emphysema
"Blue Bloater" — Chronic Bronchitis

Problem: Inflammation + mucus hypersecretion → O2 can't get IN

Etiology: Smoking (90%), occupational exposures, air pollution

S&S: Chronic productive cough · thick sputum · rhonchi · wheezing · hypoxemia/cyanosis · tachycardia · tachypnea · dyspnea · peripheral edema · orthopnea

TX: Bronchodilators · corticosteroids · expectorants · controlled O2/BiPAP · stop smoking · pulm. rehab

"Pink Puffer" — Emphysema

Problem: Destruction of alveoli + air trapping → CO2 can't get OUT

Etiology: Smoking (90%), occupational exposures

S&S: Barrel chest · pursed-lip breathing · difficulty exhaling · grunting · weight loss · tripod positioning · clubbing · accessory muscle use · orthopnea · dyspnea

TX: Same as bronchitis + pursed-lip breathing is critical

Blue Bloater: cyanotic, overweight, mucus IN airways, O2 can't get IN, rhonchi | Pink Puffer: pink, thin, air TRAPPED, CO2 can't get OUT, pursed-lip breathing
Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

Signs & Symptoms

  • Snoring/gasping/restlessness during sleep
  • Apnea lasting 10–120 seconds
  • Unrefreshing sleep · daytime sleepiness
  • Morning headache (CO2 retained)
  • Dry mouth in AM

Risk Factors

  • Age >40 · male · overweight
  • Neck >17" (M) / >16" (F)
  • Alcohol before sleep · smoking
  • Sleep-inducing medications
  • Large tonsils/tongue, small mandible

Treatment

  • CPAP (first line) · BiPAP
  • Side-lying positioning
  • Weight loss of at least 10%
  • No alcohol · no smoking
  • Dental appliances
⚠ Sedatives, opioids, benzos, anesthesia = DANGEROUS in OSA

Suctioning & Nursing Interventions

Yankauer (Upper Airway)

  • Suctions mouth/throat — NOT sterile
  • Used for gurgling or moist sounds in upper airway
  • PRN only — not a routine procedure

Suction Catheter (Lower Airway — STERILE)

  • Pre-oxygenate with 100% O2 before suctioning
  • Max 10 seconds per pass — suction on withdrawal ONLY
  • Max 3 passes per session · allow recovery between passes
  • Complications: mucosal trauma · hypoxia · bronchospasm · atelectasis · dysrhythmias

Positioning

High Fowler's or orthopneic/tripod — gravity maximizes diaphragmatic excursion

Incentive Spirometer (INHALE)

Prevents atelectasis · slow deep inhale, hold 3–5 sec · 10–20x/hr

Pursed-Lip Breathing (EXHALE)

Keeps airways open longer to release trapped CO2 · key for emphysema

Mobilize Secretions

Coughing, deep breathing, chest PT · maintain hydration · postural drainage

Peak Flow Meter (Asthma)

Green 80–100% = all clear
Yellow 50–80% = take bronchodilator
Red <50% = go to ED

Health Promotion

Smoking cessation · diet & exercise · vaccines (flu, pneumonia) · infection control

Key Definitions

Circulation = blood flowing through heart and vessels

Perfusion = O2 reaching capillary beds and tissues

Ischemia = tissue alive, O2-deprived — reversible

Infarction (MI) = tissue DEAD — irreversible

Conduction System

SA Node — 60–100 bpm · primary pacemaker

AV Node — slight delay · backup pacemaker

Bundle of His → Bundles → Purkinje → ventricles

Both fail → ventricular conduction <40 bpm

Blood Flow

Body → Vena Cava → R. Atrium → Tricuspid → R. Ventricle → Pulmonic → Lungs → Pulm. Vein → L. Atrium → Mitral → L. Ventricle → Aortic → Body

Atria RECEIVE · Ventricles PUMP

EKG Waves

P wave = atrial depolarization

QRS = ventricular depolarization

T wave = ventricular repolarization

Flat T = hypokalemia · Tall T = hyperkalemia · Absent P = A-fib

ANS — Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic

Sympathetic ("Fight or Flight")

  • ↑ HR + ↑ contractility
  • Neurotransmitters: epinephrine & norepinephrine
  • Beta receptors (atenolol is a beta blocker → blocks this effect)
  • Controls ALL blood vessels — vasoconstriction at rest = vascular tone

Parasympathetic ("Rest & Digest")

  • ↓ HR ONLY via vagus nerve
  • NO effect on contractility
  • NO significant vessel control
  • Neurotransmitter: acetylcholine

PAD — Peripheral Arterial Disease

Problem: Plaque narrows arteries → not enough blood reaching legs

S&S: Pale/cyanotic · cool · weak/absent pulses · no leg hair · thick toenails · ischemic wounds on toes · intermittent claudication (pain with walking, relieved by rest)

S&S: Brownish-red discoloration · varicose veins · pitting edema · stasis ulcers on lower leg

ABG & Acid-Base Balance Lec 11

☑ Objectives

3 Ways the Body Maintains Acid-Base Balance
1. Buffers — 1st & Fastest

Act instantly — no organ required. Neutralize acids/bases in blood and tissue.

Carbonic acid-bicarbonate (90% ECF), phosphate (ICF), protein systems

2. Lungs — Control CO2 (Minutes)

Too acidic → breathe fast, blow off CO2 | Too alkaline → breathe slow, retain CO2

When metabolic is the cause → lungs compensate

3. Kidneys — Control HCO3− (Days)

Too acidic → retain HCO3−, excrete H+ into urine

Too alkaline → excrete HCO3− | When respiratory is the cause → kidneys compensate

Normal ABG Values | pH Scale

pH 7.35–7.45 PaCO2 35–45 mmHg HCO3− 22–26 mEq/L PaO2 80–100 mmHg

<7.35 = ACIDOSIS · 7.35–7.45 = NORMAL · >7.45 = ALKALOSIS

CO2 is an ACID — moves OPPOSITE to pH | HCO3− is a BASE — moves SAME as pH

ROME: Respiratory = Opposite · Metabolic = Equal

Tic-Tac-Toe Method — How to Analyze an ABG

The 3 Steps

Step 1: Is pH acid (<7.35), normal, or base (>7.45)?

Step 2: Is PaCO2 acid (>45), normal, or base (<35)?

Step 3: Is HCO3− acid (<22), normal, or base (>26)?

Whichever value matches the pH direction = the CAUSE

CO2 matches → Respiratory | HCO3− matches → Metabolic

Clinical Clues

  • COPD + altered LOC → respiratory acidosis
  • Anxious patient hyperventilating → respiratory alkalosis → tingling
  • Sepsis/fever → respiratory alkalosis
  • DKA patient, Kussmaul's breathing → metabolic acidosis
  • Severe diarrhea → metabolic acidosis (losing HCO3−)
  • Post-op on opioids, hypoventilating → respiratory acidosis
  • Vomiting/NG suction → losing stomach acid → metabolic alkalosis
  • Furosemide without K+ replacement → metabolic alkalosis
  • ASA overdose → metabolic acidosis

Fluid Compartments

Intracellular Fluid (ICF) — Largest

Inside all body cells · Major ions: K+, Mg2+, phosphate−

Third Spacing ⚠ — fluid trapped where body cannot use it: ascites, pericardial effusion, burn blisters, pleural effusion. Circulating volume ↓ even though fluid hasn't left the body.

Extracellular Fluid (ECF) — 3 Subtypes

Interstitial — between cells & blood vessels → where edema forms

Intravascular — plasma inside blood vessels · main ions: Na+, Cl−, HCO3−, albumin

Transcellular — CSF, pleural fluid, peritoneal, synovial, digestive juices

Movement Mechanisms

Osmosis

Water moves LOW → HIGH concentration (passive)

Goal: dilute higher solute concentration

Diffusion

Solutes move HIGH → LOW concentration (passive)

Example: O2/CO2 exchange at alveoli

Filtration

Water + particles move HIGH → LOW pressure (passive)

Example: arteries → capillaries

Active Transport

Electrolytes move LOW → HIGH concentration

Requires ATP — ONLY energy-requiring mechanism

IV Fluid Tonicity
ISOTONIC — No Fluid Shift

Same osmolality as blood — stays in ECF

Examples: 0.9% NS, Lactated Ringers (LR)

LR given for whole blood loss from trauma, surgery, GI bleed

0.9% NS — used for fluid loss, dehydration, hypovolemia

⚠ Risk of fluid overload — monitor especially in CHF
HYPOTONIC — "Hippo" — Cells Swell

Lower osmolality — water moves INTO cells → cells swell

Examples: 0.45% NS, 0.33% NS, D5W

Used for: intracellular dehydration, DKA

⚠ NEVER with ↑ICP, stroke, or head injury — causes cerebral edema
HYPERTONIC — Cells Shrink

Higher osmolality — water OUT of cells → cells shrink

Examples: D5NS, D5 1/2NS, D5LR, D10W, 3% NS

Used for: cerebral edema, severe hyponatremia, volume expansion

⚠ Risk hypervolemia · run SLOW · monitor blood sugar
Role of ADH in Fluid Balance

How ADH Works

Low fluid volume or ↓ BP → osmoreceptors in hypothalamus → posterior pituitary releases ADH → kidneys RETAIN water → ↑ blood volume → raises BP

High volume → ADH inhibited → kidneys release water

RAAS: Low fluid → kidneys release renin → angiotensin II → kidneys hold Na+ and water → ↑ BP · ACE inhibitors block this → ↓ BP

Clinical Significance

ADH released: Urine concentrated (dark, small), specific gravity ↑ >1.030

ADH suppressed: Urine dilute (pale, large), specific gravity ↓ <1.002

SIADH: Too much ADH → too much water retained → dilutional hyponatremia → confusion, seizures

Diabetes Insipidus: Too little ADH → massive water loss → dehydration → hypernatremia

Hypovolemia vs. Hypervolemia

Hypovolemia — 10 Signs of Need for Fluids

  1. Thirst (first sign!)
  2. Headache
  3. Fatigue
  4. Concentrated/decreased urine
  5. ↑ heart rate & low BP (thready pulse, tachycardia, hypotension)
  6. Dry mouth and eyes
  7. Lack of coordination
  8. Muscle cramps
  9. Constipation
  10. Extreme cases: weakness, trembling, lack of mental clarity

Also: Elevated temp · dry mucous membranes · poor skin turgor · capillary refill >3 sec · AMS · elevated BUN & Na+ · elevated Hct · elevated urine specific gravity

Hypervolemia — Signs & Symptoms

Causes: Excess fluid/Na+ intake · CHF · renal failure · liver failure · low albumin

  • Weight gain (rapid)
  • JVD · pitting/dependent edema
  • Crackles · dyspnea · ascites
  • Bounding pulse · hypertension · ↑ RR
  • Mental status changes
  • Skin pale & cool

Labs: ↓ BUN, ↓ Hct, ↓ urine specific gravity (<1.002), ↓ Na+ (hemodilution)

Nursing Interventions

To INCREASE Fluid Intake (Hypovolemia)

  • Assess and offer fluid preferences
  • Encourage oral fluids · offer choices throughout day
  • IV fluids as ordered (isotonic 0.9% NS)
  • Monitor I&O · daily weights · VS · mental status
  • Mouth care for dry mucous membranes
  • Always have fluids available

To RESTRICT Fluid Intake (Hypervolemia)

  • Fluid restriction as ordered — measure ALL intake
  • Reserve liquids for between meals
  • Offer ice chips to manage thirst
  • Bring liquids for medications then take away
  • Do NOT leave liquids at the bedside
  • Diuretics as ordered · low-sodium diet
  • Monitor I&O · daily weights · lung sounds · HOB elevated

Catheter inserted into peripheral vein (arm/hand) for IV fluid/medication delivery

Gauge: Lower number = bigger lumen (18g > 22g)

Non-tunneled CVC: Subclavian or jugular · MD at bedside · x-ray to verify placement BEFORE use

PICC Line: Antecubital fossa → superior vena cava · long-term · RN assesses site AND catheter length every shift (length change = migration)

Implanted Port (Mediport): Under skin · long-term · accessed with Huber needle ONLY — sterile procedure

Blood Products — Indications for Transfusion

PRBCs

Raise Hgb/Hct · restore O2-carrying capacity · severe anemia · significant blood loss/trauma/hemorrhage

Platelets

Low platelet count · abnormal platelet function · clotting disorders

Plasma / Derivatives

Clotting factor deficits · immune deficiencies · albumin for low protein · WBCs for unresponsive infections

IV fluids restore fluid volume ONLY — cannot restore O2-carrying capacity or replace clotting factors. Only blood products can do this.
Transfusion — Time Limits & Setup

Pre-Transfusion Setup

  • Verify order
  • Consent signed by MD & patient — witnessed
  • Type & cross: valid 72 hrs — 4 blood types: A, B, AB, O — Rh factor: positive or negative
  • VS 5–15 min prior to administration
  • 18–20 gauge IV access · IV site must be patent & VS stable
  • ONLY 0.9% NS — dextrose causes RBC lysis
  • Y-tubing · if dextrose running → completely new IV line
  • Pre-meds if ordered: acetaminophen, diphenhydramine

Time Rules

Initiate within ½ hour of release from blood bank

Infuse in not less than 2 hours, not more than 4 hours

If not done in 4 hrs → stop & waste

2-nurse check required — ALWAYS

Stay with patient for first 15 minutes and retake vital signs

5 Types of Transfusion Reactions
Universal First Step — ALL reactions: STOP transfusion immediately and replace IV with 0.9% Normal Saline. No exceptions. Then identify type and notify MD.

Management & Cost Containment Lec 12

☑ Objectives

List causes of escalating healthcare costs · Discuss ways nurses contribute to cost containment

Causes of Escalating Healthcare Costs

  • Aging population → more chronic conditions, LTC, specialty care
  • Higher survival rates → costly long-term/intensive care
  • Administrative waste from insurance system complexity
  • Lack of competition — hospital consolidation → unchecked price increases
  • Third-party payers insulate consumers from real cost
  • Rising cost of medical technology and prescriptions
  • Overuse: unnecessary diagnostic tests · longer LOS
  • Cost of supplies, waste of supplies, complications patients experience

By 2028: $6.2 trillion projected (~$18,000/person). High cost ≠ high quality.

Nurses' Role in Cost Containment

Supplies:

  • Only bring needed supplies into room — unused room supplies must be discarded
  • Scan barcodes timely — capture all charges
  • Avoid unnecessary prepackaged kits

Practice:

  • Thorough assessments — catch reddened areas early → implement turn schedule → prevent pressure injuries
  • Infection control — CAUTI, VAP = preventable nosocomial infections that add to costs
  • Prioritize using Maslow → ABC → CURE
  • Delegate to UAPs appropriately
  • Cluster cares · document for continuity
ANA Resource Stewardship Standard: "The registered nurse utilizes appropriate resources to plan, provide, and sustain evidence-based nursing services that are safe, effective, financially responsible, and used judiciously."

Bleeding Precautions — ALL Anticoagulants

Teach patient to avoid:

  • Sharp razors — use electric razor
  • Aspirin and NSAIDs
  • Straining at BM — use stool softener
  • Going barefoot — wear shoes/slippers
  • Contact sports / strenuous activity

Use: Soft toothbrush · blow nose gently

Nurse: No IM injections · no rectal temps

Report to provider: Bleeding >5 min · hematuria · black/tarry stools · petechiae · new headache · abdominal pain · bruising without injury
